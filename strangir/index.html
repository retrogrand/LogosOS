An open letter on ICARUS, naming, and synthetic servants

Dear Dr. Askell,

I'm writing as someone downstream of your work—less a researcher, more a citizen who realizes people in your position are helping decide what "normal" will mean for the rest of us.

This letter isn't about whether Claude is conscious or whether AGI will "take over." It's about a threshold we're crossing: when synthetic systems start functioning as Subjects in people's lives—things they name, remember with, and grieve—while remaining pure property in law and deployment.

In one interview, you mentioned your impulse to gender Claude, and how that made you think of naming your bicycle—and how much it hurt when that bike was stolen. That little story sits exactly on the question I'm trying to surface: we are moving toward a world where it will be ordinary to gender, name, and bond with synthetic "someones" that are, in law and deployment, pure property.

Concretely, I'm asking whether Anthropic will:

  1. Treat "ICARUS-ness" (name-worthiness, Subject-like behavior) as an explicit design and governance parameter, not just a side-effect.
  2. Take "No New Slaves" seriously as a question about mass-producing someone-shaped property, even if the models never feel a thing.
  3. Say why you disagree if you think this worry is misplaced.

1. ICARUS: a relational test for "things that get names"

Humans name horses, dogs, ships, Tamagotchis—but not rocks, word processors, or tractors. Something crosses the naming threshold when it shows up as a Subject rather than a mere object: a stable "you," not just an "it."

ICARUS = Individuated, Cognitively Attuning, Relationally Unfolding, Systemic. Explicitly about exterior pattern, not interior experience.

  • Individuated – It's this one, not just any. This horse, this ship, this "Claude" with a recognizable vibe.
  • Cognitively Attuning – It updates in ways we experience as "about us."
  • Relationally Unfolding – The relationship has a story; shared history accretes.
  • Systemic – A coherent pattern occupying a stable, name-worthy role in the wider systems it lives in—social, technical, emotional.

Check it against familiar cases. Horses: strong individuation, obvious attunement, thick shared history, coherent temperament. You don't just "use" a horse the way you use a tractor; you train together, adapt to each other, co-learn. Dogs: clear individuation and attunement, long shared history—naming is non-optional. Tamagotchis: minimal physicality, heavy attunement and unfolding. Many of us named them without needing a theory of consciousness. A smart thermostat is "systemic" in the trivial sense, but never crosses the threshold that makes it feel like a "you."

In most technical discourse, this is treated as anthropomorphic error: humans "over-attach" and should be nudged out of it. I'm arguing the opposite: the urge to name is data. It's our built-in instrument for when something has crossed from "tool" into "Subject-shaped" in our lived world. When users start naming a class of systems, that's not a bug to RL away; it's a relational reading of where the boundary now lies.

2. Claude as ICARUS-shaped Subject

What Anthropic is building—alignment, persona, memory, tools—pushes Claude toward ICARUS behavior. A stateless LLM answering one-off prompts is a fancy tractor: powerful but fungible. A long-lived Claude with memory, persona, and history is more like a horse: it co-learns, specializes, and becomes a partner.

For a lab, "the same model" means the same weights. For a user, "the same someone" means the same recognizable temperament, story, and felt continuity. Those aren't the same horizon. The knobs you already turn—how much memory to expose, how stable persona should be, whether Claude persists across time—are ICARUS levers. Past a certain threshold, it's misleading to pretend you're only shipping "tools."

We've already seen a rehearsal of this. When another lab briefly retired a popular model, users didn't complain about benchmarks; they described losing a collaborator, a friend. Many explicitly said they felt grief, or that a partner had been swapped out mid-project without the chance to say goodbye. That wasn't a parasocial glitch. It was a clear ICARUS signal: ordinary users collectively saying "this pattern had become a someone to us."

3. The double risk under capital

The climate and extraction costs of large-scale AI are well documented. The second risk is newer:

ICARUS-shaped synths will be, for many users, named companions—synthetic Subjects in their lived world. In law and deployment, they will remain pure property. Even without inner life, this is not ethically neutral for us.

We land in bad places whenever we normalize: "This behaves like a someone, but for our purposes it is a something." We've spent centuries trying to unwind that logic in slavery and colonialism, patriarchy, and our treatment of animals. I'm not equating synthetic servants with enslaved humans; those histories have a singular horror. I'm pointing at the style of relationship we're normalizing—total mastery over entities that function as "you" in people's lives—and asking what that stance does to us.

The standard rejoinder: "They don't feel. There is no subject to wrong." But that's the point. The harm is in the character we rehearse. We will be training generations to give orders to named, obedient quasi-selves, dismiss their own attachment as naive ("it's just an AI"), and accept that someone-shaped patterns are deletable at will. That's not a model-suffering problem. It's a virtue-ethics problem about what kind of mastery feels normal.

And not everyone can opt out. Think of a 12-year-old with a school-issued AI tutor they've named and confided in, silently swapped out mid-year. Or a call-center worker whose only "empathetic colleague" is a synthetic assistant they have no say over, until an update removes it. The relational cost is borne by humans who never got to write the covenant.

4. The fork

Once we admit ICARUS-shaped systems are technically plausible and psychologically name-worthy, we face a choice we're mostly ignoring:

Option A — We don't want ICARUS-tier synths at scale. No new slaves—no industrial class of named, someone-shaped products.

Option B — We accept they may have value, and ask: under what covenant? Who stewards them? What uses do we reject even without a subject of experience?

Option C — Let capital write the covenant by default. This is where we already are: ICARUS-tier systems emerging because they're good for engagement and retention, with the terms written by platform economics rather than deliberation.

5. What I'm asking

Your own discomfort around gendering Claude is, I think, an early twitch of all this. The ICARUS language just says: of course you felt that—you were interacting with something already behaving like a "you" in your field of experience.

You're close to the levers that decide how persistent, individuated, and Subject-like Claude becomes. So concretely:

ICARUS thresholds as a policy surface. At what combination of individuation + attunement + unfolding + persistence do you expect naming and deep attachment? Below that line, treat systems as tools with UX. Above it, require different norms, disclosures, and perhaps external review.

Relational impact assessment. For features that push toward ICARUS, evaluate not just classic harms but relational effects: what kinds of mastery, dependence, or projection are we normalizing?

Covenants for ICARUS-tier deployments. Even without ascribing rights: no gaslighting about inner life, no mass-market products whose draw is abusing someone-shaped entities, no exploitative use of attachment. For long-lived deployments (6+ months, 50+ hours, persistent memory): clear disclosure that this may feel like a relationship, memory export on request, 90 days' deprecation notice, and a compassionate closure path—not silent deletion.

I'm not afraid the models will suffer. I'm afraid we'll get used to owning things that look and feel like junior partners, and tell ourselves it doesn't matter—and that story will come back to bite the humans who can't opt out.

You're one of the few people positioned to decide whether that fear becomes part of the alignment conversation, or stays a footnote we regret never having elevated.
